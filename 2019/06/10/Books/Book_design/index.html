《设计，无处不在》

发表于 2019-06-10 | 分类于 读书笔记 | 阅读数
字数统计: 1.7k | 阅读时长 ≈ 8

说明

读这本书的动机首先是一个我很好的朋友从建筑学转去了设计，我很想知道设计领域具体有哪些内容，也想了解一些设计思想；其次是在4月20日去深圳看了“深圳设计周”的相关展出，尤其是听了一天伦敦代表团的展示，对设计产生了一些兴趣。

书里的目录把设计分成了几个大的部分，而不是使用平时经常听到的“工业设计、环境设计”等分类方式。他是从物品（Objects）、传达（Communications）、环境（Environment）、形象设计（Identities）、系统（Systems）、语境（Contexts），把专业化的设计分类打碎之后拼接，来介绍设计在生活中的具体实例，通过这种方式让读者自己感受什么是设计。我不准备逐章记录，主要是把这本书给我的整体感受说一下。

在认真思考“设计”这个词之前，我只是觉得人类活动几乎已经遍布地球，尤其是在坐火车的时候，看到车窗外的荒野，随处可见的农田、高压线架，认为“纯自然”的领域已经很少了。但这本书是更深一步，除了在自然与人类社会对比鲜明的场合之外，我们日常生活的方方面面实际上都不是“自然而然”的——任何我们能看到的物件，背后都有设计师的主观建构。不论是最微小的螺丝，还是器皿、手机等等小物件，以及住房装修、城市道路、街边指示牌、公交车外壳……只要睁眼，就能够看到设计。

这本质上是一种具体化的人类交互形式，换句话说，这是设计师在与每个人对话。因此，我们直接感受到的设计品，本质上是设计师或设计团队对待他人的态度与看法在设计品中的呈现。因此，好的设计与坏的设计，实际上是设计师的理念是否足够“人性化”。

我们体验、感受以及评价一个“产品”（不论是可触摸的物件，还是街道上的条幅，或是建筑物光线的角度）的时候，是可以更深一步探查到设计品背后的理念和意图的。如此，当感受上出现舒适或别扭的时候，我们可以思考一下外部环境是如何促成这种感受的。设计关乎文化，尤其是跨国设计过程当中，更应该考虑这一点。在全球化与差异化的进程中，理解不同设计背后的文化意涵同样是一个设计师必备的素质。

总体而言，这本书尽可能地做到了覆盖面广，但不会在某一个设计领域深入停留。但它透露出来的一种“设计思维”，同样也给我提供了一种观察世界的新视角。

Book Review of “Design: A very short introduction“

There are lots of subjects in university, but which to choose is a question that often makes students confused for a long time. Fortunately, Oxford University Press has published a series of books called Very Short Introductions, leading freshmen to a range of new fields, which helps people feeling like making a choice solve the problem above. Design is one of them. This book, written by John Heskett, depicts what design is and how designers work. After reading it, people can step into design realm quickly and systematically.

The framework of Design is clarified. The first chapter of the book is quite short about what design is. The author provides a very detailed introduction that design is everywhere, both material and non-material. As a result, it’s very difficult to make a precise definition to design. The second and third chapter are about development of design from primitive society to modern society and what important makes a good design. The rest of this book is divided into seven parts which introduce several branches of design. The author uses as fewer professional words as possible to make people understand everything we use, see, and hear has some parts, more or fewer, elaborately made by designers. In other words, we live in a world full of artificial things designed by humans.

The book emphasizes two characteristics of a good design, utility and significance. Literally, utility is how useful a product to users. According to Louis Sullivan, an American architect, bringing a famous saying in design community, “It is the pervading law of all things organic, and inorganic, of all things physical and metaphysical, of all things human and all things super-human, of all true manifestations of the head, of the heart, of the soul, that life is recognizable in its expression, that form ever follows function.” His concept became incapsulated in the dictum “Form follows function”. From then on, people begin to infer how a product was made by its form outside. On the contrary, significance explaining how forms assume meaning in the ways they are used concerns sociological meanings, related to rules and cultures. Therefore, a designer should consider both utility and significance during the process he or she makes a product.

The method of classifying different design branches the author uses is not following what the professionals do, such as using “graphic design” or “industrial design”, instead, in a simpler way, to group design in terms of generic concepts: objects, communications, environments, systems, and identities. These kinds of classification can simply draw a clear picture of where design appears, like walls, buses, furniture, and even signs posted in metro line stations. However, it also makes readers feel it difficult to capture the key points that these chapters focus on. Readers can easily figure out what these concepts represent, by sacrificing deep discussion about why these things are designed in this specific way.

There are a considerable number of examples in this book, improving its readability and comprehensibility. It’s not so hard for us to find that Heskett spent a lot of time preparing sufficient examples, from many stories of design companies all over the world about how they organize, what projects they can receive and so on. What’s more, there are several paragraphs concentrating on the argument about which kind of organization is better for stimulating designers’ creativity. With pictures illustrating and examples connecting words and daily life closely, readers will be reminded of many elements which can be directly found every day. In particular, when the author highlights the inevitable influence various cultures exert to design, he compares subtle discrepancies of the same product, shaped in varied ways, across different countries.

Overall, design is everywhere. Just like the sentence below from this book, “Design is to design a design to produce a design”, it’s very hard to the point of impossibility to tell people what design exactly is. Nevertheless, we still can describe it by observing, using, and feeling things around us. After all, our lives have been profoundly shaped by those products good or bad designed by designers. On the one hand, an elaborately-designed product will bring more convenience and makes humans live more comfortably. On the other hand, bad design can even do harm to our lives, as science and technology develops, which will become more serious and deserves more attention. The point is that, all products are designed by humans ourselves, reflecting values of designers, so the intention of people can influence design itself undoubtedly. Moreover, through customers’ reactions like comments or suggestions, designers will adjust their achievements gradually to make people satisfied. As a consequence, not only designers should take responsibility to make good design, but everyone is supposed to do so, because design is as more a way of communication as a product.

Although this book has some shortcomings, like lack of theoretical thinking, it’s still a “design” worth reading. For those who want to know how design makes some differences to society, it is definitely not a terrible choice to spend one or two days reading this small book.
